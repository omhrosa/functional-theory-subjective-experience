A Functional Architecture for Organism-Centered Subjective Experience

Overview
The Functional Theory of Subjective Experience (FTSE) is a naturalistic and mechanistic conceptual framework proposing that subjective experience is best understood as a consequence of the functional architecture required for biological organism regulation.
Rather than beginning with consciousness itself, FTSE begins with a more fundamental biological question:
What kind of computational architecture would we expect to emerge in a brain whose primary function is to regulate an embodied organism while operating under conditions of informational isolation?
Every biological brain exists to regulate the organism in which it is embedded. Yet it has no direct access to either the external world or the organism as objective physical reality. Everything available to the brain consists exclusively of internally generated neural information originating from sensory systems, bodily regulation, memory, and ongoing neural activity.
This combination of embodiment and informational isolation defines the central biological problem addressed by FTSE.
To regulate the organism successfully, the brain must continuously infer the organism's condition, predict future states, evaluate biological significance, prioritize competing demands, and coordinate adaptive behavior using only internally available information.
FTSE proposes that these biological constraints naturally lead to an increasingly integrated functional architecture.
The brain constructs multiple internal models representing different aspects of the organism and its interaction with the environment. As behavioral complexity increases, these models become progressively integrated into a unified Self-Model capable of coordinating organism regulation.
Crucially, FTSE proposes that the integrated Self-Model is not itself subjective experience.
Rather, it serves as the functional mechanism through which the brain organizes all behaviorally relevant information.
As this integrated architecture becomes the brain's primary operational reference frame, perception, bodily regulation, emotional valuation, prediction, memory, attention, motivation, and action become continuously organized relative to the organism itself.
The result is a continuously synthesized organism-centered functional state.
FTSE identifies this organism-centered functional state with what we ordinarily call subjective experience.
Within this framework, first-person organization is not introduced as an additional phenomenon requiring separate explanation. Instead, it is understood as the natural computational perspective imposed upon an informationally isolated biological brain responsible for regulating an embodied organism.
Accordingly, FTSE develops through the following progression:
Biological Constraints → Computational Necessity → Functional Architecture → Integrated Self-Model → Operational Reference Frame → Organism-Centered Functional State → Subjective Experience
The theory does not claim to provide a complete solution to every philosophical problem concerning consciousness.
Rather, it proposes one coherent functional architecture explaining why subjective experience may naturally arise from the computational requirements of biological organism regulation.

1. Informational Isolation and the Biological Control Problem
The central premise of FTSE is that every biological brain operates under two fundamental constraints.
First, the brain is permanently embedded within the organism it regulates.
Second, it is informationally isolated from the external world.
Although the brain controls the organism's interaction with reality, it never directly observes reality itself.
It never directly observes the body as an objective physical object.
It never directly observes other organisms or their internal states.
Instead, every interaction with the world is mediated through neural signals originating from:
	sensory systems,
	internal physiological regulation,
	memory,
	and ongoing neural activity.
Consequently, every perception, prediction, belief, and behavioral decision must ultimately be constructed from internally available information.
This is not a limitation unique to humans.
It is a defining characteristic of biological nervous systems.
The brain therefore faces a fundamental computational challenge:
How can an informationally isolated system reliably regulate the organism in which it is embedded?
Successful organism regulation requires considerably more than passive perception.
The brain must continuously:
	maintain physiological stability,
	regulate energy resources,
	avoid danger,
	exploit opportunities,
	coordinate movement,
	predict future conditions,
	evaluate uncertainty,
	prioritize competing goals,
	allocate attention,
	support learning,
	and guide adaptive behavior.
These requirements cannot be satisfied by processing independent streams of information in isolation.
Every behavioral decision depends simultaneously upon sensory information, bodily state, prediction, memory, emotional significance, and ongoing goals.
FTSE therefore proposes that increasingly integrated computation is not merely advantageous.
It is a functional necessity imposed by biological organism regulation itself.
This biological control problem forms the foundation upon which the remainder of the theory is built.

2. Functional Architecture and Internal Models
To satisfy these computational demands, the brain constructs multiple interacting internal models representing different aspects of the organism and its relationship with the environment.
These models should not be understood as anatomically isolated modules.
Rather, they represent specialized functional organizations that continuously exchange information while collectively supporting prediction, evaluation, planning, learning, and adaptive behavior.
FTSE distinguishes four principal classes of internal models:
	Experience Model
	World Model
	Body Model
	Agent Models
Each contributes different information required for successful organism regulation.
Individually, however, no single model is sufficient.
The organism does not respond to isolated representations.
Every meaningful behavioral decision depends upon information originating simultaneously from multiple models.
The importance of these models therefore lies not only in what each represents individually, but in how they interact as parts of a larger functional architecture.
The following sections describe the role of each model before examining how they become integrated into a unified organism-centered framework.

2.1 Experience Model
The Experience Model represents the organism's current functional situation.
Unlike a purely sensory representation, it integrates all information that is immediately relevant to organism regulation.
Its contents may include:
	sensory perception,
	bodily sensations,
	physiological needs,
	emotional valuation,
	motivational priorities,
	attentional state,
	memory activation,
	internally generated imagery,
	ongoing cognition,
	predicted outcomes,
	and current goals.
Accordingly, the Experience Model represents the organism's currently available functional state rather than an objective description of reality.
Importantly, FTSE distinguishes the Experience Model from the Self-Model introduced later in the proposal.
The Experience Model represents currently available behaviorally relevant information.
The Self-Model provides the integrated functional organization through which that information becomes coordinated for organism regulation.
Consequently, FTSE allows integrated experiential contents to exist even when Self-Model organization remains incomplete, unstable, or only partially developed.
2.2 World Model
The World Model represents the organism's external environment.
Its primary function is not to produce an objective reconstruction of reality, but to provide a computationally useful representation of the world for organism regulation.
It may include representations of:
	physical objects,
	spatial relationships,
	environmental dynamics,
	causal structure,
	predicted future events,
	and opportunities or potential threats.
The World Model allows the brain to anticipate environmental change, support navigation, evaluate possible actions, and guide adaptive behavior.
Like every model proposed by FTSE, its value lies not in perfectly representing reality, but in providing information that improves the organism's ability to survive and interact successfully with its environment.

2.3 Body Model
The Body Model represents the organism itself as a biological system.
Its primary function is to continuously represent the current condition, capabilities, and constraints of the organism being regulated.
It may include information concerning:
	body posture,
	movement,
	physiological regulation,
	metabolic condition,
	available energy,
	physical capabilities,
	bodily integrity,
	and available actions.
The Body Model enables the brain to regulate an embodied organism rather than an abstract information-processing system.
Importantly, not every component of this model must be consciously accessible.
Many aspects of physiological regulation operate automatically while continuously contributing information required for adaptive organism regulation.

2.4 Agent Models
When socially relevant, the brain also constructs internal models representing other organisms.
These models support prediction in complex social environments by representing information such as:
	intentions,
	behavioral tendencies,
	emotional states,
	expected interactions,
	social relationships,
	and predicted future behavior.
As social complexity increases, Agent Models become increasingly important for successful organism regulation.
They allow the organism to anticipate the behavior of others, coordinate social interaction, avoid conflict, and exploit opportunities for cooperation.

3. From Multiple Models to a Unified Architecture
The four classes of models described above provide complementary representations of different aspects of organism-environment interaction.
Individually, each contributes essential information.
Collectively, however, they introduce a new computational challenge.
The organism never responds to isolated representations.
Every meaningful behavioral decision depends simultaneously upon information originating from multiple models.
A perceived object has little significance without its predicted consequences.
A bodily signal has little meaning without the environmental context in which it occurs.
A potential threat cannot be evaluated without combining perception, bodily condition, memory, emotional valuation, prediction, and current goals.
Likewise, planning future behavior requires information originating from nearly every model simultaneously.
Consequently, the problem facing the brain is no longer simply constructing internal models.
It becomes coordinating those models into a coherent architecture capable of regulating the organism as an integrated biological system.
As behavioral complexity increases, maintaining largely independent representations becomes increasingly inefficient.
Information would require constant translation between different computational contexts, increasing processing demands while reducing behavioral coherence.
FTSE therefore proposes that biological brains naturally converge toward progressively greater functional integration.
Importantly, this integration is not introduced because consciousness requires it.
It arises because efficient organism regulation requires it.
The question therefore becomes:
What form does this increasingly integrated architecture take?

4. The Integrated Self-Model
FTSE proposes that progressive model integration naturally gives rise to a higher-order functional organization: the Self-Model.
The Self-Model is not introduced as an additional cognitive module layered upon the existing architecture.
Nor should it be interpreted as an inner observer, executive homunculus, or metaphysical self.
Instead, it represents the integrated functional organization through which the brain coordinates the information required to regulate the organism as a coherent whole.
Rather than replacing the individual models, the Self-Model organizes their interaction.
It continuously integrates information concerning:
	the organism's current functional condition,
	the body,
	the external environment,
	biologically relevant goals,
	motivational priorities,
	expected future states,
	available behavioral options,
	and socially relevant information whenever appropriate.
Importantly, the Self-Model is not simply another model among many.
It represents a qualitative transition within the architecture.
The Experience Model represents the organism's currently available functional situation.
The World Model represents the external environment.
The Body Model represents the organism itself.
Agent Models represent other organisms.
The Self-Model organizes these otherwise independent representations into a unified functional architecture capable of regulating the organism as a single integrated system.
Within FTSE, the Self-Model should therefore be understood primarily as a mechanism of integration rather than as subjective experience itself.
The distinction between these two concepts is central to the theory and becomes increasingly important in the following sections.
5. The Operational Reference Frame
The existence of an integrated Self-Model does not, by itself, solve the biological control problem.
The information contained within the Self-Model must still be organized in a way that allows the brain to regulate the organism efficiently.
FTSE proposes that this requirement naturally leads to the next stage of the architecture.
The integrated Self-Model becomes the brain's primary operational reference frame.
This distinction is fundamental.
The Self-Model is the mechanism.
The operational reference frame is the function performed by that mechanism.
An external observer can describe the organism objectively.
The brain cannot.
It has no external viewpoint from which to regulate the organism.
Every computation performed by the brain must ultimately answer a single biological question:
What does this information mean for the organism currently being regulated?
This principle applies equally to perception, bodily regulation, emotional valuation, prediction, memory, motivation, planning, and action.
Each acquires functional significance only through its relationship to the organism.
From this perspective, first-person organization is no longer introduced as an independent mystery.
Instead, it becomes the natural computational perspective adopted by an informationally isolated biological control system.
The brain does not organize computation around the organism because consciousness requires it.
It organizes computation around the organism because effective regulation requires it.
FTSE therefore proposes that the operational reference frame is not an additional feature layered upon cognition.
It is the natural computational consequence of regulating an embodied organism under conditions of informational isolation.

6. Continuous Functional Organization
Once the operational reference frame has been established, organism regulation becomes a continuous process of functional organization.
The brain is not merely storing representations.
It is continuously organizing behaviorally relevant information into a coherent organism-centered structure.
As new information becomes available:
	predictions are updated,
	perception is reinterpreted,
	emotional significance changes,
	behavioral priorities shift,
	physiological regulation adapts,
	memory modifies expectations,
	attention reallocates computational resources,
	and ongoing goals are continuously re-evaluated.
Every component continuously influences every other component.
Perception affects prediction.
Prediction shapes attention.
Attention influences perception.
Emotion modifies behavioral priorities.
Behavior generates new sensory information, beginning the cycle again.
Rather than operating as a collection of independent computational modules, the architecture functions as a continuously interacting system.
Its defining characteristic is not continuous observation.
Nor continuous representation.
It is the continuous organization of behaviorally relevant information required for adaptive organism regulation.
Within FTSE, this ongoing organization represents the brain's normal mode of operation.

7. The Organism-Centered Functional State
The continuous organization of behaviorally relevant information produces a unified organism-centered functional state.
This state is not statically stored.
It is continuously synthesized and continuously updated as both the organism and its environment change.
At any given moment it reflects:
	the organism's physiological condition,
	current environmental circumstances,
	predicted future events,
	emotional significance,
	motivational priorities,
	behavioral readiness,
	ongoing cognitive activity,
	and the organism's continuously changing relationship with the surrounding world.
Importantly, this functional state does not represent reality from an objective perspective.
It represents reality as it currently matters to the organism.
Every element derives its significance from its relationship to the organism's biological regulation.
This distinction is central to FTSE.
The organism-centered functional state is not simply another representation.
It is the continuously integrated computational context within which the brain regulates the organism.
It represents the current functional organization of everything that is behaviorally relevant at that moment.
FTSE proposes that this state is the natural outcome of the functional architecture developed throughout the preceding sections.
Only after establishing this architecture does the theory return to the original question of subjective experience.

8. Subjective Experience
Having established the proposed functional architecture, FTSE now returns to the question from which the discussion began.
What is subjective experience?
FTSE does not propose that subjective experience is an additional phenomenon generated after neural computation has been completed.
Nor does it propose that consciousness depends upon a specialized "consciousness module."
Instead, FTSE advances an identity claim.
The continuously synthesized organism-centered functional state generated through the operation of the integrated Self-Model constitutes what we ordinarily identify as subjective experience.
This distinction is fundamental.
The theory does not propose that the Self-Model itself is subjective experience.
Nor does it propose that subjective experience is produced by the Self-Model.
Instead:
	the Self-Model is the mechanism of integration,
	the operational reference frame is the mode of computation,
	the organism-centered functional state is the continuously generated result,
	and subjective experience is identified with that resulting functional state.
Within this framework, subjective experience is not something added to biological computation.
It is the organism-centered mode in which biological computation is continuously organized while regulating an embodied organism.
From this perspective, first-person experience no longer appears as an unexplained additional property.
It becomes the natural consequence of the functional architecture required for efficient organism regulation under conditions of informational isolation.
FTSE therefore proposes that subjective experience is best understood not as a separate entity requiring its own mechanism, but as the continuously synthesized organism-centered functional state through which an informationally isolated brain regulates the organism it is embedded within.
9. The Role of Emotion
Within FTSE, emotions are not treated as secondary additions to cognition or as independent mental faculties.
Instead, they function as organism-level valuation mechanisms.
Their primary role is to continuously evaluate the biological significance of both current and predicted conditions relative to the organism.
Put simply, emotions answer a fundamental regulatory question:
How important is this information for the organism right now?
Fear evaluates immediate danger.
Pain evaluates bodily damage or potential injury.
Pleasure evaluates biologically favorable conditions.
Curiosity evaluates opportunities for learning and exploration.
Interest evaluates potentially valuable information.
Anxiety evaluates uncertainty and possible future threats.
These evaluations continuously influence:
	attention,
	prediction,
	memory,
	planning,
	behavioral prioritization,
	physiological regulation,
	and action selection.
Consequently, emotions do not merely accompany subjective experience.
They actively shape the organism-centered functional state by determining the biological significance of ongoing information.
Within FTSE, emotional valuation is therefore an integral component of organism regulation rather than an independent psychological process.

10. Gradations of Subjective Experience
FTSE does not treat subjective experience as an all-or-none phenomenon.
Instead, the theory proposes that organism-centered functional organization may exist at different levels of complexity.
As nervous systems evolve greater computational capacity, several characteristics are expected to develop progressively:
	richer internal models,
	stronger model integration,
	more accurate prediction,
	greater temporal continuity,
	improved behavioral flexibility,
	increasingly stable organism-centered organization.
Consequently, different organisms may exhibit different degrees of subjective experience without requiring fundamentally different explanatory principles.
Likewise, altered neurological or psychological conditions may modify the organization of the organism-centered functional state without requiring an entirely separate account of consciousness.
Within this framework, subjective experience is expected to vary continuously with the sophistication and stability of the underlying functional architecture.
This perspective is consistent with the gradual evolution of biological nervous systems.

11. Altered States
Because FTSE identifies subjective experience with organism-centered functional organization rather than with a particular neural structure, altered states can be interpreted as alterations of that organization.
Sleep, dreaming, anesthesia, neurological injury, meditation, psychedelic states, and psychiatric conditions may each modify different aspects of the architecture.
Some conditions primarily affect:
	model integration,
	body representation,
	emotional valuation,
	prediction,
	attentional organization,
	temporal continuity,
	or behavioral prioritization.
Although the underlying mechanisms may differ considerably, FTSE predicts that changes in subjective experience should generally correspond to changes in the organization of the organism-centered functional state rather than to the activation or deactivation of a single "consciousness center."
This interpretation remains compatible with the distributed nature of contemporary neuroscience.

12. Predictions and Empirical Implications
FTSE is primarily intended as a conceptual framework rather than a detailed neuroscientific implementation.
Nevertheless, it generates several broad empirical expectations.
If subjective experience corresponds to organism-centered functional organization, systematic alterations of that organization should systematically alter subjective experience.
More specifically, FTSE predicts that:
	disrupting communication between major functional models should reduce the coherence of subjective experience,
	disrupting the operational reference frame should impair organism-centered organization even when some cognitive abilities remain relatively preserved,
	alterations in emotional valuation should directly modify subjective experience rather than merely accompany it,
	disturbances affecting body representation should alter first-person organization,
	and improvements in large-scale functional integration should generally increase the coherence and stability of subjective experience.
These predictions remain intentionally broad.
FTSE does not attempt to specify detailed neural implementations or individual experimental paradigms.
Instead, it provides a functional framework capable of generating increasingly specific hypotheses as empirical neuroscience advances.

13. Relationship to Existing Theories
FTSE is broadly compatible with many established findings in neuroscience while differing in its explanatory emphasis.
The theory agrees that:
	subjective experience depends upon neural activity,
	large-scale functional integration is essential,
	prediction plays a central role in cognition,
	embodiment fundamentally shapes cognition,
	and biological regulation provides the context in which cognition evolved.
Its principal distinction lies in the question from which it begins.
Many contemporary theories ask:
How does neural activity generate consciousness?
FTSE instead asks:
What functional architecture would biological evolution be expected to produce for an informationally isolated brain responsible for regulating an embodied organism?
Within this framework:
	internal models provide specialized representations,
	the Self-Model integrates those representations,
	the operational reference frame organizes computation around the organism,
	continuous functional organization maintains a unified computational context,
	the organism-centered functional state represents the current integrated state of organism regulation,
	and subjective experience is identified with that continuously synthesized functional state.
Accordingly, FTSE should not be understood as a competing neural implementation.
Instead, it is proposed as a higher-level functional architecture within which findings from predictive processing, embodied cognition, systems neuroscience, computational neuroscience, and related approaches may be interpreted as complementary rather than mutually exclusive.
14. Scope and Limitations
FTSE is intentionally presented as a functional and conceptual framework rather than a complete neuroscientific theory of consciousness.
Its objective is not to identify every neural mechanism involved in subjective experience, nor to resolve every philosophical question concerning consciousness.
Instead, FTSE proposes a coherent explanatory framework connecting:
	biological constraints,
	computational necessity,
	functional architecture,
	organism-centered information processing,
	and subjective experience.
Accordingly, FTSE does not claim:
	to identify a single neural location responsible for consciousness,
	to provide a complete neural implementation of every proposed functional component,
	to explain every altered state of consciousness,
	to resolve every philosophical debate concerning qualia,
	or to provide a definitive criterion for determining consciousness in biological or artificial systems.
Likewise, FTSE does not claim that subjective experience has already been reduced to currently understood neurobiology.
Instead, it proposes a functional architecture whose value should ultimately be judged by its conceptual coherence, biological plausibility, explanatory power, compatibility with empirical evidence, and ability to generate testable hypotheses.
The proposal is therefore intended as a framework for further discussion, criticism, refinement, and empirical investigation rather than as a completed theory.

15. The Hard Problem
The traditional Hard Problem asks:
Why should physical processes give rise to subjective experience at all?
FTSE does not claim to solve this problem in the strongest philosophical sense.
Instead, it proposes that the question may be approached from a different direction.
Rather than beginning with subjective experience itself, FTSE begins with the biological requirements imposed upon an informationally isolated brain responsible for regulating an embodied organism.
From these constraints, the theory derives a functional architecture in which organism-centered information processing becomes a computational necessity rather than an arbitrary design choice.
The central proposal is therefore an identity claim.
If subjective experience is identical to the continuously synthesized organism-centered functional state produced by this architecture, then the explanatory task changes.
Rather than explaining how neural computation generates an additional phenomenon called experience, the task becomes explaining why biological evolution converges upon this particular mode of functional organization.
Whether this reframing ultimately succeeds remains an open scientific and philosophical question.
FTSE therefore should not be understood as claiming to dissolve the Hard Problem.
Instead, it proposes an alternative explanatory perspective grounded in biological function.

17. Conclusion
The Functional Theory of Subjective Experience proposes that subjective experience cannot be understood independently of the biological function the brain evolved to perform.
An informationally isolated brain must continuously regulate the embodied organism in which it exists.
To accomplish this, it constructs multiple internal models representing different aspects of the organism and its interaction with the environment.
As behavioral complexity increases, these models become progressively integrated into a unified Self-Model capable of coordinating organism regulation.
The integrated Self-Model functions as the brain's operational reference frame, organizing all behaviorally relevant information relative to the organism itself.
This continuous process of organism-centered organization generates a unified functional state describing the organism's current biological situation.
FTSE proposes that this continuously synthesized organism-centered functional state is what we identify as subjective experience.
Within this framework, first-person experience is not treated as an additional phenomenon layered upon neural computation.
Instead, it is interpreted as the natural functional organization through which an informationally isolated biological brain regulates an embodied organism.
Whether this proposal ultimately proves correct remains an empirical question.
The purpose of FTSE is therefore not to claim that the problem of consciousness has been solved, but to offer a coherent, biologically grounded, and mechanistically motivated framework capable of generating new discussion, theoretical refinement, and future empirical investigation.
