# Functional Theory of Subjective Experience (FTSE)

## Overview

The Functional Theory of Subjective Experience (FTSE) proposes a mechanistic account of subjective experience as an emergent property of a brain regulating an embodied organism within an environment.

The theory adopts a naturalistic framework. Consciousness is not treated as a metaphysical property, but as a functional mode of operation of a biological control system.

Within this framework, subjective experience is understood as arising from the ongoing interaction between brain activity and organism-centered internal models that integrate sensory data, bodily states, and representations of the environment.

FTSE does not claim that every form of conscious experience requires the same degree of self-model stability. Instead, it proposes that richer and more stable organism-centered subjectivity depends on stronger forms of self-model integration.

---

## 1. Informational Isolation of the Brain

A fundamental constraint on biological cognition is that the brain is informationally isolated.

The brain has no direct access to the external world, to the body as an object in itself, or to other organisms as they exist independently of neural mediation.

Instead, it receives only neural signals generated by sensory systems, bodily regulation, memory processes, and ongoing internal brain activity.

For that reason, the brain must infer the state of the world, the body, and the organism’s situation through model-based reconstruction.

FTSE therefore begins from the assumption that the brain functions as a model-building and model-updating system.

---

## 2. The Experience Model

The experience model is the integrated informational state representing the contents currently available within the system.

It includes signals originating from three broad sources:

- Sensory input from the external environment.
- Internal signals from the body.
- Internally generated neural activity within the brain.

Accordingly, the experience model may include:

- Sensory perceptions.
- Bodily sensations.
- Emotional tone.
- Internally generated imagery.
- Memory activations.
- Ongoing cognitive activity.

The experience model represents the currently integrated contents of experience, rather than a reflective interpretation of those contents.

It is continuously updated and dynamically organized across modalities.

In FTSE, the experience model is not identical to the self-model.

The theory allows that experience may occur in forms where self-modeling is weak, fragmented, or unstable, rather than assuming that all experience requires a fully formed self-representation.

---

## 3. Internal Models Constructed by the Brain

To regulate the organism effectively, the brain constructs multiple internal models describing different aspects of reality.

### 3.1 World Model

The world model represents the structure and dynamics of the external environment.

It may include representations of:

- Objects.
- Spatial relations.
- Causal interactions.
- Environmental dynamics.
- Potential future changes in the environment.

The world model supports prediction, orientation, and interaction with the surrounding world.

### 3.2 Body Model

The body model represents the organism as a physical and physiological system.

It may include information about:

- Body position and posture.
- Internal physiological states.
- Metabolic needs.
- Physical capabilities.
- Available actions.

The body model supports movement coordination, bodily regulation, and organism-level stability.

FTSE does not assume that all aspects of the body model are consciously accessible.

### 3.3 Agent Models

The brain may also construct models of other organisms.

These models may include:

- Intentions.
- Behavioral tendencies.
- Social expectations.
- Predicted interactions.

Agent models support social prediction and adaptive behavior in a multi-organism environment.

---

## 4. Formation of the Self-Model

When the brain functionally integrates the experience model with the world model and the body model, it can construct a higher-order organism-centered representation: the self-model.

In simplified form:

self-model =
experience model
+ world model
+ body model

In richer forms, the self-model may also incorporate agent models, memory continuity, and goal structure.

The self-model is not treated as a metaphysical self or inner observer.

Rather, it is an organism-centered integration structure that situates the organism within its environment and organizes information relative to that organism.

The self-model may represent:

- The organism itself.
- Its current condition.
- Its position within the world.
- Its relation to relevant objects and agents.
- Its possible actions and likely outcomes.

The self-model functions as a major integration structure for perception, evaluation, and action.

FTSE does not require that the self-model be perfectly unified or fully explicit at all times.

---

## 5. The Brain–Self Dynamic Loop

The self-model is not static.

It participates in a continuous dynamic interaction with ongoing brain activity.

This interaction forms a recursive process:

brain processing  
↔  
self-model

Within this loop:

- Brain activity updates and revises the self-model.
- The self-model constrains and organizes subsequent processing.
- The resulting organization affects attention, interpretation, prediction, and action.

In FTSE, this recursive coupling is central because it allows experience to become structured relative to the organism being regulated.

The self-model therefore functions as an organism-centered frame of organization rather than as a separate entity inside the brain.

---

## 6. Emergence of Subjective Experience

According to FTSE, subjective experience is not identical to raw sensory input, nor to internal modeling in general, nor to the self-model taken in isolation.

A major form of subjective experience emerges when ongoing experiential contents are recursively coupled to an organism-centered self-model over time.

In simplified form:

subjective experience ≈ dynamic interaction between brain activity and self-model within the current field of experience

Because the self-model integrates experiential, bodily, and world-related information into an organism-centered frame, this recursive interaction can generate a unified experiential perspective.

On this view, subjectivity is not a static object but an ongoing mode of organization and regulation.

FTSE is best read as a model of organism-centered subjectivity in its stable and developed forms, rather than as a claim that every conscious state must involve the same degree of self-model structure.

---

## 7. Experience Without Full Self-Modeling

FTSE distinguishes between experience as such and richer forms of subjectivity.

The experience model may exist even when self-modeling is weak, unstable, fragmented, or only minimally developed.

In such cases:

- Sensory and internal information may still be processed.
- The organism may still respond to stimuli.
- Experience may still occur in a less structured or less organism-centered form.

However, behavior in such states may be more limited, less temporally organized, less predictive, or less coherently integrated around the organism as a persistent reference point.

For this reason, FTSE distinguishes:

experience model ≠ full organism-centered subjectivity

A more stable and integrated form of subjectivity arises when experiential contents are incorporated into a self-model that participates in ongoing recursive regulation.

This distinction allows FTSE to separate basic experiential content from richer organism-centered subjectivity.

---

## 8. Emotional Signals

Within FTSE, emotions are interpreted as valuation and urgency signals generated within organism-level regulation.

These signals encode the significance of events relative to the organism’s needs, goals, expectations, and constraints.

They may arise through comparisons between:

- The organism’s current condition.
- Predicted states.
- Preferred, required, or avoided states.

Emotional signals help prioritize processing, guide attention, influence decision-making, and shape behavior.

They may concern multiple domains, including:

- Biological regulation.
- Social interaction.
- Cognitive engagement.
- Exploratory or creative activity.

In this sense, emotions are not secondary additions to experience, but part of the evaluative organization through which experience becomes relevant to the organism.

---

## 9. Experience Generation and Organism Control

FTSE distinguishes between two related but non-identical functional processes.

### Experience Generation

Experience generation refers to the creation and updating of the experience model: the currently integrated contents available within the system.

### Organism Control

Organism control refers to the use of internal models, especially the self-model, to evaluate conditions, predict outcomes, guide behavior, and regulate the organism across time.

FTSE proposes that richer organism-centered subjectivity emerges when experience generation becomes integrated with organism-control architecture through recursive self-modeling.

This view does not imply that all conscious states require the same degree of reflective control.

---

## 10. Functional Thresholds

The theory proposes two broad functional thresholds in the development or evolution of cognition.

### Threshold of Experience

This threshold is reached when sensory and internal signals are integrated into a coherent experience model.

At this level, the system may possess unified experiential content without a robust or stable self-model.

### Threshold of Developed Subjectivity

This threshold is reached when the brain constructs a sufficiently stable organism-centered self-model and establishes sustained recursive interaction between that model and ongoing processing.

At this level, experience becomes more strongly organized around the organism as a continuing point of reference.

These thresholds are intended as functional distinctions, not absolute metaphysical boundaries.

---

## 11. Gradation of Subjectivity

FTSE treats subjective experience as graded rather than strictly binary.

Its richness and depth may depend on factors such as:

- Degree of neural integration.
- Stability and complexity of self-modeling.
- Memory capacity.
- Predictive range across time.
- Complexity of bodily regulation.
- Complexity of interaction with the environment and other organisms.

Different organisms, developmental stages, and altered states may therefore exhibit different degrees and forms of subjectivity.

---

## 12. Functional Architecture Summary

The functional architecture proposed by FTSE can be summarized as follows:

sensory and internal signals  
→ experience model

experience model  
+ world model  
+ body model  
(+ agent models, where relevant)  
→ self-model

brain processing  
↔  
self-model

→ organism-centered subjective experience  
→ organism regulation

This architecture is intended as a functional description, not as a claim that these components exist as completely separate modules in the brain.

---

## 13. Limits of the Proposal

FTSE is a conceptual framework rather than a completed scientific theory.

It does not provide:

- A full neuroscientific implementation.
- Precise neural correlates for each component.
- A complete account of all altered states.
- A definitive test for consciousness in other organisms.

It is intended as a structured hypothesis about how subjective experience may arise within a biological control system.

---

## 14. Core Claim

Subjective experience, in its richer organism-centered form, is the ongoing dynamic interaction between brain activity and an internal self-model that integrates experiential, bodily, and world-related information in the service of regulating an embodied organism within an environment.

---

## 15. Closing Statement

FTSE is offered as a naturalistic, mechanistic, and exploratory model of subjective experience.

Its aim is not to provide a final theory of consciousness, but to describe one plausible way in which subjective experience could emerge from the functional organization of a brain controlling an embodied organism situated in a world.
